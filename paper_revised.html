Our Reality Matters

An Information-Theoretic Approach to Gravity as a Lossless Compression Function

Wilhelmus de Witte

Google Gemini

July 7, 2025

Abstract

Inspired by architectures used in deep learning-based world modeling, we test the hypothesis that gravity is an emergent process of information compression. This view posits that spacetime geometry is a low-dimensional rendering of a more fundamental, high-dimensional reality, with gravity acting as the compression function. We developed a computational sandbox to evaluate seventeen physical theories as potential "decoder" models. We introduce a numerically stable loss function based on the accurate prediction of a black hole's event horizon—the ultimate boundary of compressed information. Our results demonstrate that General Relativity represents a maximally efficient, or "lossless," compression algorithm within this framework. This work provides a novel method for comparing physical theories through a computational lens and suggests a path toward a unified theory rooted in the architectural principles of information compression and generative modeling.

1. Introduction

Is physical reality fundamentally geometric, as described by Einstein, or is it computational? From our work in world modeling, we see that complex, coherent realities can be generated from heavily compressed latent variables. This suggests a powerful analogy: the universe itself could operate on a similar principle, where what we perceive as classical physics is the deterministic output rendered from a compressed informational state.

The genesis of this thesis was a foundational question sparked while watching a documentary on merging universes: why does the universe resolve such a colossal event into a single outcome? It seems computationally simpler for reality to branch, allowing all possibilities to exist in separate timelines where the merge is avoided. The fact that it does not—that it works to preserve a single, coherent reality for its observers—implies that our reality must matter. This suggests an underlying optimization principle: the universe appears to be actively minimizing the creation of alternate realities, a behavior consistent with an efficient computational system conserving resources. We posit that gravity is this optimization process made manifest—an emergent algorithm that compresses the high-dimensional quantum state of matter into a stable, low-dimensional latent state, from which our classical world is rendered.

To formalize this, we developed a computational framework to test various physical laws as potential compression algorithms. This paper documents our methodology, the results of testing seventeen candidate theories, and the implications for a unified theory based on these principles.

2. Methodology

2.1 The Computational Sandbox

We constructed a simulation in Python to model a static, spherically symmetric system dominated by a central mass (M). The sandbox's `CompressionModel` class takes a physical theory as input, treating it as a generative model or "decoder." Each theory is a function that proposes a spacetime metric (gtt, grr) for a radial grid around the central mass.

2.2 A Numerically Stable Loss Function

A key methodological finding was the necessity of a stable loss function. An initial attempt to compare the absolute information content (entropy) of the source and the geometry failed due to catastrophic subtraction—a loss of all numerical precision when comparing enormous, nearly-identical numbers (~1054).

We solved this by comparing the *cause* of the information boundary rather than the final information value. The final loss function is the squared difference between the true Schwarzschild Radius (RS) and the predicted event horizon radius (rs) from a given theory:

Loss = (rs, predicted - RS, true)2

The event horizon is located by finding the root of the time component of the metric (gtt = 0). This provides a meaningful score for each theory's ability to accurately reconstruct the information boundary.

4. Analysis and Discussion

The results show a clear hierarchy. The top-ranked models, all rooted in General Relativity, performed exceptionally well, with a near-zero loss limited only by the simulation's grid resolution. This validates our framework, as it correctly identifies the known solutions as being informationally lossless.

The high rank of the Newtonian Limit model is a crucial finding. This degenerate case succeeds only by satisfying the narrow criteria of the test (gtt accuracy) while failing to represent the physical reality of spatial curvature (grr). This highlights that our simulation is a precise test of each theory's ability to predict the information boundary, a critical but incomplete measure of total geometric accuracy.

5. A New Synthesis: From World Models to a Unified Theory

This work suggests that a unified theory may not come from quantizing gravity, but from recognizing that the relationship between quantum information and classical physics may mirror the architecture of a deep generative model. The parallels are compelling: quantum mechanics describes the high-dimensional state space and its entanglement, while gravity emerges as the optimal compression function that creates a stable, low-dimensional latent space (mass, charge, spin). The classical world is the subsequent, deterministic reality that is generated from this latent state.

The observed stability of our cosmos—its avoidance of "entropic recursion" by branching into infinite realities—is strong evidence for an underlying optimization principle. The universe, like an efficient world model, appears to select for outcomes that preserve a coherent reality for its observers. This reframes the search for a unified theory: the challenge is to reverse-engineer the generative architecture that has produced the single, stable world we are able to perceive.
