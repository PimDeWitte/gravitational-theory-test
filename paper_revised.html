An Information-Theoretic Test of Gravitational Theories as Compression Functions

Wilhelmus de Witte

Abstract

Inspired by architectures used in deep learning-based world modeling, we test the hypothesis that gravity is an emergent process of information compression. This view posits that spacetime geometry is a low-dimensional rendering of a more fundamental, high-dimensional reality. To test this, we developed a two-phase computational framework. The first phase evaluated sixty-nine gravitational theories against a static loss function based on the accurate prediction of a black hole's event horizon. While this validated the approach, it revealed a degeneracy where geometrically incomplete theories performed deceptively well. To resolve this, we developed a second, more robust test based on a dynamic consequence: the geodesic trajectory of a test particle. This dynamic test correctly penalizes incomplete models and provides a more discerning measure of a theory's fidelity. Our results confirm that General Relativity represents a maximally efficient compression algorithm and demonstrate a powerful methodology for evaluating physical theories through a computational, information-theoretic lens.

1. Introduction

Is physical reality fundamentally geometric, as described by Einstein, or is it computational? From our work in world modeling, we see that complex, coherent realities can be generated from heavily compressed latent variables. This suggests a powerful analogy: the universe itself could operate on a similar principle, where what we perceive as classical physics is the deterministic output rendered from a compressed informational state.

The genesis of this thesis was a foundational question sparked while watching a documentary on merging universes: why does the universe resolve such a colossal event into a single outcome? It seems computationally simpler for reality to branch, allowing all possibilities to exist in separate timelines where the merge is avoided. The fact that it does not—that it works to preserve a single, coherent reality for its observers—implies that our reality must matter. This suggests an underlying optimization principle: the universe appears to be actively minimizing the creation of alternate realities, a behavior consistent with an efficient computational system conserving resources. We posit that gravity is this optimization process made manifest—an emergent algorithm that compresses the high-dimensional quantum state of matter into a stable, low-dimensional latent state, from which our classical world is rendered.

2. Methodology

2.1 Phase 1: Static Horizon Prediction

Our first experiment was a large-scale computational sandbox designed to test sixty-nine gravitational theories. The core methodology involved treating each theory as a "decoder" that generates a spacetime metric. We then scored each theory using a numerically stable loss function based on the squared difference between the true Schwarzschild Radius and the predicted event horizon radius:

Lossstatic = (rs, predicted - RS, true)2

This test was designed to evaluate a theory's ability to correctly place the information boundary of a black hole.

2.2 Phase 2: Geodesic Trajectory Analysis

The static test revealed a critical limitation: it produced a "false positive" for the Newtonian Limit model, which has an incorrect spatial geometry. To resolve this ambiguity, we developed a more rigorous experiment based on a dynamic physical consequence: the trajectory of a test particle. This approach tests how the curvature of both space and time in a given model affects motion.

The methodology is as follows:

  1. Define a Ground Truth: We first calculate the trajectory of a test particle released from rest in a pure Schwarzschild spacetime (General Relativity).
  2. Simulate Candidate Trajectories: We then simulate the particle's path using the spacetime metrics generated by other candidate theories.
  3. Calculate Trajectory Loss: The new loss function is the squared physical distance between the final position of the particle as predicted by a candidate theory and its final position in the ground truth trajectory.

A model can only score well on this test if it correctly reproduces the combined effects of both spatial curvature (grr) and time dilation (gtt) on a moving object.

4. A New Synthesis: From World Models to a Unified Theory

This work suggests that a unified theory may not come from quantizing gravity, but from recognizing that the relationship between quantum information and classical physics may mirror the architecture of a deep generative model. The parallels are compelling: quantum mechanics describes the high-dimensional state space and its entanglement, while gravity emerges as the optimal compression function that creates a stable, low-dimensional latent space (mass, charge, spin). The classical world is the subsequent, deterministic reality that is generated from this latent state.

The observed stability of our cosmos—its avoidance of "entropic recursion" by branching into infinite realities—is strong evidence for an underlying optimization principle. The universe, like an efficient world model, appears to select for outcomes that preserve a coherent reality for its observers. This reframes the search for a unified theory: the challenge is to reverse-engineer the generative architecture that has produced the single, stable world we are able to perceive.
