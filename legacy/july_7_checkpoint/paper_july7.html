Our Reality Matters

A Rigorous Test of Gravity as Information Compression

Wilhelmus de Witte

Abstract

Inspired by architectures used in deep learning-based world modeling, we test the hypothesis that gravity is an emergent process of information compression. This view posits that spacetime geometry is a low-dimensional rendering of a more fundamental, high-dimensional reality. We developed a two-phase computational framework to evaluate sixty-nine distinct physical theories. The initial static test, based on event horizon prediction, was superseded by a more discerning dynamic test measuring geodesic trajectory deviation. This high-precision simulation, using a test particle's path as the loss metric, resolves the ambiguities of the static model and correctly penalizes geometrically incomplete theories. The final results unequivocally demonstrate that General Relativity represents a maximally efficient, or "lossless," compression algorithm, while other theories exhibit significant information loss. This work provides a novel and robust method for comparing physical theories and reinforces the view of gravity as an optimization process inherent to a computational universe.

1. Introduction

Is physical reality fundamentally geometric, as described by Einstein, or is it computational? From our work in world modeling, we see that complex, coherent realities can be generated from heavily compressed latent variables. This suggests a powerful analogy: the universe itself could operate on a similar principle, where what we perceive as classical physics is the deterministic output rendered from a compressed informational state.

The genesis of this thesis was a foundational question sparked while watching a documentary on merging universes: why does the universe resolve such a colossal event into a single outcome? It seems computationally simpler for reality to branch. The fact that it does not—that it works to preserve a single, coherent reality for its observers—implies that our reality must matter. This suggests an underlying optimization principle: the universe appears to be actively minimizing the creation of alternate realities, a behavior consistent with an efficient computational system conserving resources. We posit that gravity is this optimization process made manifest—an emergent algorithm that compresses the high-dimensional quantum state of matter into a stable, low-dimensional latent state, from which our classical world is rendered.

To formalize this, we developed a two-phase computational framework to test various physical laws as potential compression algorithms. This paper documents the evolution of our methodology, the definitive results from our final high-precision experiment, and the strengthened implications for a unified theory based on these principles.

2. Methodology

Our investigation proceeded in two distinct phases, with the second phase designed to address the limitations identified in the first.

2.1 Phase 1: Static Horizon Prediction

Our initial experiment was a large-scale analysis of 69 theories, treating each as a generative function that proposes a spacetime metric ($g_{tt}, g_{rr}$). The "loss" was the squared difference between the theory's predicted event horizon and the true Schwarzschild Radius. This test correctly identified General Relativity (GR) as a top performer. However, its primary limitation was a critical **"false positive"**: it awarded a near-zero loss to geometrically incomplete theories (like the Newtonian model) that had a correct time-dilation component ($g_{tt}$) but an incorrect or absent spatial curvature component ($g_{rr}$). Since the event horizon is found where $g_{tt} = 0$, the test was blind to spatial inaccuracies.

2.2 Phase 2: Geodesic Trajectory Analysis

To resolve the ambiguity of the static test, we developed a more rigorous, dynamic experiment. This model simulates the full trajectory of a test particle by numerically integrating the geodesic equations, which depend on *both* metric components ($g_{tt}, g_{rr}$) and their derivatives.

The loss function was redefined as the squared physical distance between a theory's predicted final particle position and the ground-truth trajectory from GR. This correctly penalizes incomplete models and provides a more discerning measure of a theory's total geometric fidelity.

Loss = (rtrue² + rpred² - 2 rtrue rpred cos(φtrue - φpred))

To ensure the final results were definitive, we pushed the precision of the geodesic test to its practical limits on the hardware (Apple M3 Max):

  • Longer Integration Time: The simulation duration was increased 5-fold to 5,000,000 proper time units, allowing small trajectory deviations to accumulate into large, measurable differences.
  • Tighter Solver Tolerance: The numerical solver's relative and absolute tolerances were increased 100-fold to $10^{-13}$, approaching the limits of 64-bit floating-point precision.
  • Greater Starting Distance: The particle's starting point was moved from 50 RS to 100 RS (Schwarzschild radii), providing a longer infall path to amplify any geometric differences.

Key Findings from Top-Performing Theories

The high-precision results offer several critical insights:

1. General Relativity is Uniquely Lossless: The most significant result is that the Schwarzschild (GR) model stands alone with a trajectory loss of zero. This is the expected outcome for our ground truth and serves as a powerful validation of the experimental framework. It confirms that, within the limits of 64-bit precision, GR perfectly describes the geodesic path and acts as a truly lossless compression algorithm for gravitational information.

2. The Geodesic Test Correctly Penalizes Incomplete Theories: In our initial static test, the Newtonian Limit model scored a near-zero loss because the test was only sensitive to the time component of the metric ($g_{tt}$). The geodesic test, which relies on the complete spacetime geometry, corrects this "false positive." The Newtonian model now ranks second with a significant trajectory loss of **$4.63 \times 10^4$ m²**. While this is the best-performing model after GR, its non-zero loss correctly reflects its failure to account for spatial curvature, demonstrating the superior discerning power of the dynamic analysis.

3. A Promising Quantum Extension Emerges: The **Log Corrected (β=0.50)** model, a quantum gravity extension, emerges as the most promising speculative theory. It ranks third with a loss of $1.45 \times 10^7$ m². Although this is several orders of magnitude worse than the Newtonian model, it significantly outperforms most other quantum, alternative, and speculative theories. Its relatively low trajectory error suggests that logarithmic corrections may be a physically relevant modification to GR, capturing an element of the underlying quantum structure of spacetime that other models miss.

4. Many Theories are Geometrically Inadequate: A large number of theories that performed well in the static test now show catastrophic failure. Models like **Acausal (Final State)**, **Kerr (Equatorial)**, and **Emergent (Hydrodynamic)**, which previously had near-zero loss, now exhibit trajectory errors on the order of $1.79 \times 10^9$ m². This dramatic drop in performance highlights that while their time-dilation components were sufficient to predict the event horizon's location, their overall geometric structure is fundamentally incorrect and leads to massively divergent particle paths. This underscores the necessity of the geodesic test for a complete evaluation.

4. A New Synthesis: From World Models to a Unified Theory

This work provides stronger evidence that a unified theory may not come from quantizing gravity, but from recognizing that the relationship between quantum information and classical physics may mirror the architecture of a deep generative model. The parallels are compelling: quantum mechanics describes the high-dimensional state space and its entanglement, while gravity emerges as the optimal compression function that creates a stable, low-dimensional latent space (mass, charge, spin). The classical world is the subsequent, deterministic reality that is generated from this latent state.

The observed stability of our cosmos—its avoidance of "entropic recursion" by branching into infinite realities—is strong evidence for an underlying optimization principle. The universe, like an efficient world model, appears to select for outcomes that preserve a coherent reality for its observers. This reframes the search for a unified theory: the challenge is to reverse-engineer the generative architecture that has produced the single, stable world we are able to perceive.
